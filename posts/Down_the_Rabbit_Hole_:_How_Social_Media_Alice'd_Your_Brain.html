Down the Rabbit Hole: How Social Media Alice'd Your Brain

October 30, 2025

Welcome to Wonderland, Population: You

Congratulations! You've fallen down the rabbit hole, and unlike Alice, you didn't even need to drink a mysterious potion. You just opened Instagram at 9 PM to "check one thing" and suddenly it's 2 AM and you're watching a video about a guy in Thailand who trains squirrels to paint miniature landscapes. How did you get here?

The answer, dear reader, is that you've been Alice'd. And before you Google that term expecting to find a Wikipedia page, let me save you the trouble: it's a persuasion technique as old as Carroll's tale itself, weaponized by Silicon Valley with the efficiency of a well-oiled algorithm.

What in the Mad Hatter's Name is "Alice in Wonderland" Persuasion?

The Alice in Wonderland technique, also known as "confusion technique" in psychological circles, works by deliberately creating a state of disorientation and uncertainty. When you're confused, your brain desperately searches for something—anything—that makes sense. And whoever provides that anchor of clarity? Well, they've got you.

Here's how it worked in Carroll's story:

  • Alice shrinks and grows without warning
  • The rules of reality keep changing (cats disappear but their smiles don't, because sure, why not)
  • Characters speak in riddles and nonsense
  • Nothing is quite what it seems
  • Authority figures are absurd but still authoritative

Sound familiar? It should. Because every time you open a social media app, you're stepping through the looking glass.

How Social Media Turned You Into Alice (The Corporate Version)

The Infinite Scroll: Where Does It End? (Spoiler: It Doesn't)

Remember when websites had pages? Quaint, wasn't it? Now you have an infinite feed that changes every time you refresh it. You can never see it all. You can never reach the bottom. It's like the Queen's croquet ground where the balls are hedgehogs and the mallets are flamingos—nothing stays in place long enough for you to make sense of it.

The confusion this creates is intentional. When you can't predict what's coming next, your brain releases dopamine in anticipation. You keep scrolling because maybe—just maybe—the next post will be the one that makes it all worthwhile. (It won't be. But hey, keep scrolling!)

The Algorithm: Your Personal Cheshire Cat

The Cheshire Cat appeared and disappeared at will, offering cryptic advice that may or may not have been helpful. Sound like anyone you know? Perhaps a certain algorithmic entity that decides what you see based on calculations you'll never understand?

The algorithm knows things about you that you don't know about yourself. It shows you content based on patterns you weren't aware you had. One minute you're watching cooking videos, the next you're deep into conspiracy theories about ancient civilizations, and you have no idea how you got there. The path is invisible. The logic is opaque. But boy, does it keep you engaged!

The Ever-Changing Rules of Engagement

In Wonderland, Alice had to play croquet with impossible equipment under rules that changed mid-game. On social media, you have to navigate:

  • Mysteriously changing algorithms that bury your posts without explanation
  • Features that appear and disappear (RIP Fleets, Circles, and whatever else died this week)
  • Terms of service that update faster than you can read them
  • Shadow bans that may or may not exist depending on who you ask
  • Engagement metrics that fluctuate wildly for reasons known only to the machine gods

You're playing a game where the rules are secret, constantly changing, and enforced by an invisible authority. The Queen of Hearts would be so proud.

The "Eat Me, Drink Me" Content Trap

Alice consumed things that made her shrink and grow unpredictably. You consume content that makes your worldview expand and contract in equally disorienting ways. In the span of ten minutes, you can experience:

  • Outrage at a news story (probably missing context)
  • Envy over someone's vacation photos (definitely staged)
  • Inspiration from a motivational quote (likely misattributed)
  • Anxiety about a trending disaster (may or may not be real)
  • Amusement at a cat video (finally, something authentic)

Your emotional state becomes as unstable as Alice's size. And in that confusion, you're malleable. Suggestible. Ready to click on whatever offers clarity or comfort.

Why You're Absolutely Defenseless (You Poor, Sweet Summer Child)

Let's be brutally honest: you're up against some of the smartest people on Earth, armed with more behavioral data than has ever existed in human history, backed by billions in funding, with a singular goal of keeping your eyeballs glued to a screen.

They Have the Science, You Have... What Exactly?

Social media companies employ:

  • Behavioral psychologists who understand conditioning better than you understand your own habits
  • Neuroscientists who know which parts of your brain light up and how to make them light up more
  • Data scientists who can predict your behavior before you know what you're going to do
  • UX designers who spend months perfecting the shade of red on a notification dot

Meanwhile, you have... willpower? Good luck with that.

Your Brain is Hopelessly Outmatched

Your brain evolved to scan the savanna for lions, not to resist algorithmically optimized dopamine delivery systems. It's like bringing a spear to a drone strike. Your ancient neural circuitry doesn't stand a chance against A/B tested engagement optimization.

The confusion technique works because your brain craves patterns and predictability. When those are deliberately disrupted, your executive function goes offline and your more primitive responses take over. You become reactive instead of reflective. You scroll instead of think.

The Network Effect Prison

Even if you wanted to quit, good luck convincing your friends, family, colleagues, and that person you met once at a party but now feel obligated to stay connected with. Social media has achieved what the Mad Tea Party only dreamed of: a gathering that never ends, that you can never truly leave, because leaving means social death.

Your defense mechanisms are useless when the prison is made of social connections.

Fighting Back: A Laughably Incomplete Guide to Digital Self-Defense

Now for the part where I pretend there are easy solutions! (There aren't, but let's go through the motions, shall we?)

Awareness: The First Step to Still Probably Failing

Congratulations on reading this far! You're now aware that you're being manipulated. Does that help? Not really! But here's what you can try:

  • Recognize the confusion state: When you feel disoriented or emotionally volatile while scrolling, that's not accidental. Name it. "Ah yes, I'm being Alice'd right now."
  • Question the rabbit holes: When you find yourself deep in content you didn't intend to consume, pause and ask: "How did I get here?" Retrace your steps. See the manipulation.
  • Notice the pattern disruptions: Pay attention to how feeds change, how content appears in seemingly random orders. The randomness is designed.

Setting Boundaries: LOL Good Luck

Sure, you could try these, but let's be real about your chances:

  • Time limits: Set them. Break them. Feel guilty. Repeat. (The circle of digital life!)
  • Turn off notifications: Okay, this one actually works. That red dot is literally weaponized against your prefrontal cortex.
  • Scheduled checking: Decide you'll only check social media at specific times. Fail within 3 hours. Try again tomorrow.
  • Physical barriers: Keep your phone in another room while working. Then go get it because you "need to check something important." (You don't.)

Algorithmic Resistance: Fighting Fire with Indifference

The algorithm wants engagement. Deny it. Here's how to be the most boring user ever:

  • Don't react immediately: See something outrageous? Wait. That emotional response is what feeds the beast.
  • Curate ruthlessly: Unfollow, mute, and block like your sanity depends on it (it does). The algorithm can't Alice you if you control the guest list to your tea party.
  • Click with intention: Every click is data. Every like is training. Be deliberate about what you engage with, or the algorithm will decide for you.
  • Poison the well: Deliberately engage with content outside your bubble. Confuse the confusion algorithm. Like recipes, then philosophy, then carpentry videos. Be unpredictable.

Reality Anchors: Remember the Real World Exists (Barely)

Ground yourself in non-digital reality:

  • Physical activities: Do things that require your hands and eyes in the real world. The algorithm can't follow you to the garden. (Yet.)
  • In-person connections: Talk to humans face-to-face. Remember what that's like? No? Exactly.
  • Long-form content: Read books. Watch full movies. Engage with things that have beginnings, middles, and ends. Your brain needs narrative structure to fight the chaos.
  • Boredom: Embrace it. Let yourself be bored. Boredom is your brain's natural state, and it's when you do your best thinking. The constant stimulation is the problem, not the solution.

The Nuclear Option: Digital Minimalism

For the truly brave (or desperate):

  • Delete apps from your phone (keep them browser-only, where they're clunkier and less addictive)
  • Use website blockers during work hours
  • Have a dumb phone for calls and texts, a separate device for internet
  • Actually delete your accounts (lol, as if you would)

Will you do any of this? Probably not. The rabbit hole is comfortable once you stop struggling.

The Uncomfortable Truth: You'll Probably Stay in Wonderland

Here's the thing about Alice in Wonderland—at the end, Alice wakes up and realizes it was all a dream. But you? You're not dreaming. This is real. The manipulation is real. The psychological exploitation is real. The billions in profit from your attention are very real.

And yet, you'll probably keep scrolling. Because the alternative—disconnection, missing out, being outside the conversation—feels worse than being manipulated. The social media companies know this. They're counting on it.

The Mad Hatter asked Alice, "Why is a raven like a writing desk?" The answer, according to Carroll, is that there isn't one. It's a riddle designed to confuse. And you're living in a world of riddles designed to keep you confused, engaged, and profitable.

So What Now?

You've read this entire article. You understand the manipulation. You recognize the techniques. You see the rabbit hole for what it is.

And in five minutes, you'll probably open Instagram.

Because awareness isn't enough. Knowledge isn't enough. You're up against systems designed by the brightest minds, funded by infinite money, optimized through billions of data points, and refined through years of psychological exploitation.

But hey—at least now when you're doom-scrolling at 2 AM, you'll know why. And that's something. Right?

Right?
