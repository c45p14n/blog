Why Nothing Ever Works As It Should

November 23, 2025
"In this world, wherever there is light there are also shadows. As long as the concept of winners exists, there must also be losers. The selfish desire of wanting to maintain peace causes wars, and hatred is born to protect love."

The Fundamental Problem: Reality Versus Human Purpose

We face a truth that has haunted thinking beings since consciousness first emerged: the basic structure of reality itself opposes human intention, purpose, and design. This is not just a practical observation about things going wrong. It is a deep condition that runs through the entire fabric of existence. The world as we experience it presents itself as a systematic rejection of human will, an eternal opposition to everything we try to accomplish, a cosmic structure specifically built to undermine every attempt we make at creating order and meaning.

Heidegger spoke of our existence as fundamentally defined by death we are beings moving toward our end, and this fact shapes everything. But there is another equally fundamental condition: we are beings moving toward failure. Every project we start contains within itself the seeds of its own destruction. Every system we build carries the mechanisms of its eventual collapse from the very beginning. Every plan we make includes the precise ways it will fall apart. This is not pessimism. This is simply describing what we can observe. Malfunction is not an exception to how things work it is the basic state of reality itself.

The Ancient Wound: Everything Changes in the Worst Possible Way

The ancient Greek philosopher Heraclitus said that you cannot step into the same river twice everything is always changing, always in flux. But we have misunderstood this teaching. The eternal change of the universe is not just a neutral description. It is an indictment against stability, against permanence, against the very possibility of reliable structures. Every moment of existence violently displaces what came before. Every present arrangement gets annihilated. Everything we build gets swept away by the merciless movement of time.

The world does not simply change randomly. It changes specifically in ways that invalidate our preparations. Our strategies become useless precisely when we have perfected them. Our understanding deepens only to reveal new layers of complexity that make our previous knowledge inadequate. We adapt to circumstances, and the circumstances mutate into something that demands completely new adaptation. This is not evolution toward improvement. This is a race against reality itself, and we are structured to lose this race forever.

Schopenhauer's Vision: The Universe at War With Itself

Arthur Schopenhauer saw to the heart of existence when he identified the Will as the fundamental force underlying all reality a blind, purposeless striving that has no goal. But his analysis needs to go further. The Will is not merely blind and without purpose. It is actively hostile to the individual manifestations of will that emerge from it. We are expressions of a cosmic force that does not care at all about our particular projects, our specific desires, our individual survival. We are the universe trying to organize itself, and the universe fundamentally opposes organization.

Every human effort is Will turning against itself. We are consciousness struggling to impose order on the very forces that created consciousness only to undermine it. We build structures physical, social, conceptual, institutional and the same energies that allowed their construction guarantee their eventual destruction. Creation and destruction are not separate processes. They are simultaneous aspects of the same movement. To build is to begin the process of collapse. To plan is to create the conditions for disappointment. To hope is to prepare ourselves for suffering.

Sisyphus and the Absurd: Worse Than We Thought

Albert Camus gave us the myth of Sisyphus as the perfect image of human existence. Sisyphus is condemned to roll a boulder up a mountain forever. Every time he reaches the top, the boulder rolls back down, and he must start again. Camus said we must imagine Sisyphus happy finding meaning not in success but in the struggle itself. This is profound, but it does not capture the full horror of our situation.

At least Sisyphus has consistency. His boulder behaves in predictable ways. The mountain remains stable. The rules of his punishment stay fixed. We face something far worse. Our boulder changes its weight while we are pushing it. The mountain reshapes itself as we climb. The rules governing success and failure shift without warning or pattern. We are condemned not just to endless meaningless labor, but to labor whose very rules are unstable, whose methods prove unreliable, whose goals disappear or transform as we approach them. The gap between human beings who need meaning and a universe that provides none is not just empty space. It is active opposition between our structure building nature and reality's structure destroying nature.

Entropy: The Universal Law of Breakdown

The second law of thermodynamics states that in any closed system, disorder always increases. This is not merely a physics equation about heat and energy. It is a fundamental truth about the direction of everything. The universe moves toward disorder, toward the breakdown of organized structures, toward the dissolution of complexity into uniform chaos. Every instance of order is temporary, costs enormous energy to maintain, and is destined to fall apart.

We are order creating beings in an order destroying universe. Every human achievement is a local, temporary reversal of this universal trend, and it requires constant maintenance, continuous energy, perpetual vigilance against decay. Civilizations rise through monumental effort and collapse through the mere passage of time. Knowledge accumulates through generations of labor and can be lost in a single catastrophe. Institutions built over centuries can fail in moments. The asymmetry is absolute and crushing: destruction requires no effort, while creation demands everything we have, and destruction always wins in the end.

What "Working" Really Means

What we call something "working properly" is merely a statistical anomaly, a brief and fragile arrangement of matter and energy that temporarily resists the pull toward chaos. Functionality is not the default state. It is an achievement constantly threatened by the basic physics of the universe. We do not live in a world where things work and occasionally break. We live in a world where everything is always breaking, and through constant, exhausting effort, we occasionally maintain the illusion of stability for brief moments.

The Trap of Time: Understanding Comes Too Late

Søren Kierkegaard identified a cruel paradox about human existence: life must be lived forward, but it can only be understood backward. We are condemned to make decisions without knowledge, to act on incomplete information, to commit ourselves to paths whose consequences remain hidden until it is far too late to change direction. Understanding arrives as a form of torture not as useful knowledge we can apply, but as hindsight about precisely where and how we failed.

This structure of time ensures that we are always acting blind, groping through possibilities we cannot fully see, making choices whose results will only become clear after they have become permanent facts we cannot undo. The future is hidden not by accident but by necessity. If we could see forward with clarity, we would be paralyzed by the certainty of failure. Ignorance is not something we can fix. It is the requirement for taking action at all. We must act without knowledge, and we will gain knowledge only when it can no longer help us.

Progress: Creating Better Problems

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel saw history as a process of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis contradiction resolving itself into higher forms. But there is a terrible truth here: synthesis does not resolve contradiction so much as elevate it to a higher level of complexity. Every solution we find generates new problems. Every advance creates new forms of suffering. Every achievement of reason reveals new horizons of unreason that we did not know existed.

We do not progress toward harmony. We progress toward increasingly sophisticated forms of dysfunction. Our systems grow more complex, and thus our potential failures multiply exponentially. Our societies become more intricate, and thus their vulnerabilities proliferate geometrically. Our knowledge deepens, and thus we become aware of ever deeper layers of ignorance. Progress does not end in resolution. It ends in absolute complication, an infinite regress of problems generating larger problems generating even larger problems without end.

The Paradox of Development

Every human capacity, when we develop it, turns against itself. Language enables communication and creates misunderstanding. Reason reveals truth and generates rationalization. Freedom allows self determination and produces paralyzing anxiety. Power permits action and breeds corruption. There is no human ability that, when fully realized, does not also realize its own opposite and negation.

The Death of Cosmic Order: Nietzsche's Revelation

Friedrich Nietzsche proclaimed "God is dead," meaning that there is no longer any transcendent ground for meaning and value in the universe. What follows from this is not freedom but the terrifying recognition that there exists no cosmic principle guaranteeing that things "should" work in any particular way. The universe is not a rational system but chaos temporarily constrained by physical laws. There is no providence, no ultimate purpose, no inherent direction toward which events naturally move.

In such a universe, the question "why doesn't this work as it should?" reveals itself as meaningless. There is no "should." There are only forces, causes, conditions, and constraints. Things happen according to principles that govern matter and energy, principles that are completely indifferent to human needs, desires, plans, or expectations. To rage against things not working is to rage against the structure of physical reality itself a futile act, yet perhaps the only honest response available to conscious beings trapped in a cosmos that cares nothing for consciousness.

The Will Against Itself

Nietzsche's concept of the Will to Power describes the fundamental human drive to expand our influence, to overcome resistance, to impose our interpretation on the world. But will encounters not just resistance but systematic negation. Reality is not merely resistant to will. It is structured to frustrate it completely. Every assertion of power meets greater opposing powers. Every attempt at mastery reveals deeper layers of what we cannot master. We discover that strength exhausts itself, that affirmation requires endless renewal, that meaning creation is always haunted by the specter of meaninglessness waiting to reassert itself the moment we stop actively maintaining meaning.

Kafka's Nightmare: Systems That Cannot Be Navigated

Franz Kafka understood modern existence as few others have. His characters find themselves subject to vast, incomprehensible systems that operate according to hidden logic, that promise justice or resolution or access while systematically denying it, that present themselves as rational and organized while functioning as nightmares of arbitrary power. K. seeks to reach the Castle and never does. Josef K. is put on trial for an unnamed crime by a court that cannot be located. Gregor Samsa transforms into an insect and his family adapts by erasing him from their lives.

These stories are not symbols of specific social conditions. They are descriptions of existence itself. We operate within systems we did not choose, cannot fully understand, and cannot escape. These systems claim to work according to rules, but the rules are contradictory, hidden, or applied arbitrarily. We are told there are procedures for success, methods for achieving goals, pathways to resolution. We follow these procedures and they lead nowhere. We employ these methods and they fail. We pursue these pathways and they circle back on themselves. The promise of functionality is the lure that keeps us engaged with systems whose actual purpose is not to work but to perpetuate themselves through our frustrated engagement with them.

Condemned to Freedom: Sartre's Burden

Jean Paul Sartre declared that we are "condemned to be free," that we bear total responsibility for what we make of ourselves. This freedom is not a gift but a curse. We are thrown into existence without choosing it, forced to make choices without adequate knowledge, held responsible for outcomes we could not foresee, judged by standards we did not create.

But the problem goes deeper: we are responsible not just for our choices but for navigating a world structured to make successful choice impossible. We must act decisively while radically uncertain. We must commit ourselves completely while knowing our commitment may prove disastrous. We must take responsibility for consequences that emerge from factors beyond our knowledge or control. Freedom is not the power to determine outcomes. It is the obligation to choose despite the certainty that our choice will prove inadequate. We are held responsible for our failure to achieve what could never have been achieved.

Stoic Acceptance: Wisdom or Surrender?

The ancient Stoics Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, Seneca taught acceptance of what lies beyond our control, distinction between external events and our judgments about them, focus on virtue rather than outcomes. This philosophy presents itself as wisdom but functions as a strategy of resignation. To accept that things do not work as they should is to normalize dysfunction, to abandon any expectation of justice or coherence, to internalize the betrayal of human purpose by cosmic indifference.

Marcus Aurelius wrote his philosophy while presiding over an empire in decline, facing plague and invasion and political instability. His philosophy of acceptance was forged in catastrophe and counsels surrender disguised as serenity. To distinguish what we can control from what we cannot is to recognize that we control almost nothing our thoughts, our responses, but not the world, not other people, not circumstances, not consequences. The Stoic sage achieves peace by accepting powerlessness. This is not wisdom but survival strategy in a hostile universe.

The Inner Fortress

Epictetus, born a slave, taught that no external force can touch the inner freedom of the mind. But this inner fortress is not liberation it is retreat. When the world proves systematically unworkable, we withdraw into our own consciousness and declare our suffering to be merely a matter of perspective. This is psychological violence disguised as enlightenment. To say that our distress over things not working is just a judgment we can revise is to place the burden of cosmic dysfunction on individual minds, to make our suffering our own fault for failing to achieve sufficient detachment from reality.

Buddhism's Radical Solution: Eliminate Desire Itself

Buddhism identifies craving and desire as the root of suffering, of the pervasive sense that things are not as they should be. The Buddhist solution is extreme: eliminate desire itself. Achieve enlightenment through the extinction of the self that desires. If there is no one to want things to work differently, then their not working cannot produce suffering.

This diagnosis is accurate and horrifying. Things do not work as they should precisely because "should" is a category created by consciousness that desires, and reality does not organize itself according to the preferences of temporarily organized matter. But the solution the elimination of desire is the elimination of everything that makes existence recognizably human. To not desire is to not care, to not strive, to not hope, to not imagine alternatives, to not project ourselves into possible futures. It is not enlightenment but extinction, not liberation but negation of the self.

Built to Be Unsatisfied

Modern psychology discovers what Buddhism knew: we are structured to be perpetually unsatisfied. We achieve goals and immediately establish new ones. We adapt to improvements until they no longer feel like improvements. This is not a flaw but a fundamental feature of consciousness itself, a mechanism that keeps organisms striving rather than satisfied. We are built to want, and therefore built to suffer from the inevitable gap between wanting and having, between how things are and how we need them to be.

The Best of All Possible Hells: Voltaire's Satire

Voltaire destroyed the philosophy of optimism through the character of Dr. Pangloss, who maintains that we live in "the best of all possible worlds" even as disasters accumulate into absurdity. The philosopher Leibniz had argued that God, being perfectly good and rational, must have created the best possible universe from among all alternatives. Voltaire revealed this as obscene rationalization of suffering, an intellectual defense of the indefensible.

But we have not learned this lesson. We continue to generate explanations for why things not working is actually optimal. Systems fail, and we are told they are self correcting. Institutions prove dysfunctional, and we are assured there are no better alternatives. We live not in the best of all possible worlds but in the only world possible, and we mistake necessity for justification.

Cosmic Indifference: Worse Than Hostility

The ancient philosopher Epicurus taught that the gods exist in perfect tranquility, utterly unconcerned with human affairs. This was meant to free humanity from religious fear. Instead, it reveals the structure of reality: the forces that govern existence physical laws, causal necessity, statistical probability are fundamentally indifferent to human purposes. The universe is not hostile to us. Hostility would imply attention, relationship, engagement. The universe is worse than hostile. It is indifferent.

Our projects unfold in a cosmos that takes no notice of them. Our values have no correspondence to any cosmic principle. Our suffering produces no response from reality itself. We cry out, and the void does not even acknowledge our voice. Things do not work as they should because "should" is a human category projected onto a reality that knows nothing of need, nothing of purpose, nothing of us at all.

Power Creates Dysfunction: Foucault's Discovery

Michel Foucault showed that power is not just repressive but productive it creates subjects, knowledge, and the very categories through which we understand ourselves. But power also produces dysfunction as a mechanism of control. Systems that do not work keep populations dependent on those who promise to fix them. Complexity that cannot be navigated ensures the necessity of expert mediation. Problems that resist solution justify endless intervention.

We live in societies where malfunction is not an anomaly but a governing principle. Institutions are designed not to achieve their stated purposes but to perpetuate their own existence. Processes are not optimized for results but for the generation of dependency. The promise of functionality is the lure that maintains engagement with systems whose actual purpose is not to work but to reproduce the conditions of their own necessity. We do not live in broken systems that need repair. We live in systems whose brokenness is their operating principle.

Technology's Trap: Control That Creates Chaos

Martin Heidegger warned against understanding technology as just neutral tools we use for our purposes. Technology, he argued, is a way of revealing the world that transforms everything into resources available for optimization and exploitation. But this revealing conceals something essential: the more we approach the world technologically, the more we encounter systematic resistance, unintended consequences, emergent complexity that defies control.

The technological worldview promises mastery and delivers entanglement in systems too complex to understand, too interconnected to modify without cascading failures, too optimized for specific conditions to adapt when conditions change. We have built a world of exquisite precision and thereby created brittleness, fragility, vulnerability to any deviation from expected parameters. Our very attempts to ensure things work as they should have generated entirely new categories of malfunction that did not exist before.

Malfunction as Foundation: Derrida's Insight

Jacques Derrida's philosophy reveals that what appears supplementary or external to a system is often what makes the system possible. Malfunction is not external to function it is the condition that makes function possible. We define working properly only in relation to not working. We establish norms only by identifying deviations. We create standards only through experience of failure to meet them. The "should" that things violate is generated by the violation itself.

This suggests a radical conclusion: there is no ideal state of functionality that malfunction represents a falling away from. There is only ongoing process, variation, deviation, difference. What we call "working properly" is a temporary stabilization of forces that are already changing, a momentary balance in systems that tend toward imbalance. Malfunction is not the exception but the norm function is exceptional, purchased through constant effort, destined to decay, defined only in relation to its own negation.

The Ancient Intuition: Reality as Hostile Design

The ancient Gnostics believed that the material world was created not by a good god but by an incompetent or malicious false god who botched creation. This mythology captures something phenomenologically true: the world appears structured specifically to frustrate human flourishing. Not randomly, not accidentally, but systematically, as if designed to ensure that our highest aspirations meet intractable obstacles, that our deepest needs remain unsatisfied, that our most careful preparations prove inadequate.

We need not believe Gnostic theology to recognize the truth of their observation. Whether through malevolent design, cosmic indifference, or the mechanical operation of physical law, the result is identical: we inhabit a reality that structurally opposes the realization of human purpose. Things do not work as they should because they work precisely as they must given the nature of matter, energy, entropy, and time none of which care about "should."

Conclusion: Living With the Truth

We arrive at an uncompromising recognition: nothing works as it should because there is no transcendent standard of "should" to which reality must or will conform. We are temporary organizations of matter that have developed the capacity to imagine alternatives, to project ideals, to formulate expectations and we exist in a universe utterly indifferent to these imaginings. The gap between how things are and how we need them to be is not a temporary problem awaiting solution but the fundamental structure of conscious existence in a material cosmos.

Every philosophy, every wisdom tradition, every system of thought that has confronted this reality has offered some response: Stoic acceptance, Buddhist extinction of desire, Existentialist embrace of absurdity, Nietzschean creation of meaning through will. None of these responses resolve the underlying condition. They are strategies for surviving it, ways of managing the suffering inherent in being a purpose seeking creature in a purposeless universe.

The rage this produces is justified. The desperation is warranted. We are beings structured to need coherence, reliability, justice, and meaning, cast into a cosmos that provides none of these as givens. We must create them through effort, maintain them through vigilance, watch them decay despite our attempts at preservation. This is not a temporary condition that better systems will remedy. This is the permanent situation of consciousness itself.

And yet we persist. We build knowing things will break. We plan knowing plans will fail. We hope knowing hope will be disappointed. Perhaps this persistence is humanity's defining characteristic not our rationality, not our creativity, not our capacity for love, but our stubborn refusal to accept what we nevertheless clearly perceive: that we are not made for this world, and this world is not made for us, and the mismatch is absolute and eternal.

Nothing ever works as it should. This is not pessimism but description. This is not cynicism but clarity. This is not defeat but the only honest starting point for any genuine reckoning with existence. And from this starting point, having abandoned illusions of cosmic justice or reliable functionality, perhaps we can begin to construct something real not a solution, but a way of standing upright in the ruins of expectation, clear eyed about the nature of the world we inhabit and defiant in our determination to impose temporary order on permanent chaos, knowing full well it will not last, doing it anyway, for no reason other than that we are beings who cannot do otherwise.

The universe offers no consolation. Philosophy provides no resolution. And still we continue, because continuation is all we have, and because somewhere in the gap between cosmic indifference and human meaning making, in the space between what is and what should be, we discover not comfort but something more valuable: the terrible, beautiful, enraging truth of what it means to be conscious in a cosmos that makes no provision for consciousness, to care in a universe structured around indifference, to build knowing everything built will eventually fall.

This is not wisdom. It is witness. It is the recognition that we stand as conscious beings in an indifferent cosmos, aware of our condition, enraged by its injustice, and determined to continue anyway not because we will succeed, but because the attempt itself is the only response to existence that honors both the truth of our situation and the dignity of human consciousness that refuses to surrender even when surrender would be rational.

Nothing works as it should. And we persist in the face of this truth. That persistence, that defiance, that refusal to accept what cannot be changed perhaps that is all the meaning we will ever have, and perhaps it is enough.
