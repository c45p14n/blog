Why I’m Leaving Major Social Medias — and the SHADY part of the Internet

October 11, 2025

I’m done playing in someone else’s living room. After years of using major social platforms and relying on closed-source , corporate-controlled hardware and software, I’ve chosen to remove my presence from mainstream social media and actively reduce my footprint on the open internet. This isn’t panic — it’s a deliberate decision based on several recurring realities that have made online life feel unsafe, opaque, and fundamentally out of my control.

Proven risks of proprietary, un-auditable systems

Many of the services and devices we rely on are built on proprietary code and sealed firmware that users can’t inspect or control. That means critical parts of my digital life run inside black boxes: Closed-source social platforms centralize huge amounts of personal data and choose how it’s stored, analyzed, and shared — often without clear, user-friendly control. Device firmware (from Wi‑Fi chips to system controllers) and proprietary OS components can include telemetry, undocumented features, or security holes that users and independent researchers can’t fully audit. When a subsystem is closed and privileged, the only parties that can realistically find and fix deep problems are the vendor and a small set of researchers — not the people who depend on the devices. That lack of auditability removes both control and trust. If I can’t verify what runs on the hardware that holds my data or mediates my communications, I don’t want to rely on it.

The Intel Management Engine and similar “always-on” subsystems

One concrete example that crystallized my decision is the family of privileged subsystems built into modern hardware — the Intel Management Engine (ME), Converged Security and Management Engine (CSME), and vendor equivalents. Key concerns: These are essentially separate microcontrollers running firmware with deep access to memory, peripherals, networking, and sometimes the ability to operate while the main OS is powered down. The code is proprietary and cryptographically locked to vendor-signed images, so users cannot replace or fully audit it. Over the years researchers and incident responders have found serious vulnerabilities in ME/CSME and in related out‑of‑band management technologies (AMT, BMCs, etc.). Some vulnerabilities allow remote attackers or local attackers with physical access to gain highly privileged execution. Real-world actors — from advanced criminal groups to state-linked operators — have shown interest in firmware and management-engine-level compromises because they’re stealthy and persist across reinstalls.

in simple words: owning the firmware layer means having a backdoor-level capability. Whether those subsystems were designed with hostile intent or for legitimate manageability, their presence and documented vulnerabilities make me uneasy about placing trust and personal data on platforms that depend on them.

Evidence of targeted surveillance and exploitation

  • Large-scale leaks, disclosed vulnerabilities, and reported exploits show two things: Intelligence agencies and criminal groups both pursue access to core hardware and firmware. Public reporting and forensic work have repeatedly shown attackers aiming below the OS to maintain stealth.
  • When privileged, proprietary subsystems are compromised, the attack surface expands dramatically — attackers can persist, tamper with logs, or exfiltrate data even if the main OS is reinstalled. Those incidents aren’t hypothetical. They shifted my threat model from “someone scraping my profile” to “powerful actors can observe or control my device in ways regular users can’t detect or fix.”

    • social platforms themselves amplify risk

    Even without exotic firmware attacks, mainstream social media centralizes metadata, content, and social graphs. Effects include:

    Highly detailed targeting capability for advertisers, political actors, and bad actors. Large-scale data collection, retention, and sharing that can be subpoenaed, leaked, or abused. Automated systems and opaque moderation/algorithmic decisions that shape visibility and can expose private information unpredictably.

    For me, the combined risk of centralized data collection plus the invisible hardware/firmware layer is unacceptable.

    Practical harms I want to avoid

    Permanent digital traces of sensitive conversations, relationships, and opinions. Identity profiling that feeds advertising, surveillance, or coercive actions. The possibility of undetectable compromise via firmware or management subsystems. Being forced to rely on corporations or vendors to remediate deep technical failures.

    What I’m doing instead

    I removed myself from major social networks and began the following, focused on minimizing dependency on closed systems:

  • Deleted or deactivated accounts on major platforms; requested data removal where possible.
  • Migrated essential communication to tools where I control the endpoint (end‑to‑end encrypted messengers on devices I manage) and limited metadata exposure. (eg. Matrix)
  • Replaced or minimized use of hardware with known un-auditable subsystems where feasible (using devices and vendors with stronger transparency, or older hardware without those features).
  • Hardened remaining devices: firmware/BIOS updates from trusted vendors, disabled out‑of‑band features when possible, and minimized unnecessary network services. (with LibreBoot you can disable IME)
  • Adopted a “need-to-know” sharing posture: fewer public profiles, less personal data published, and using throwaway contact points for nonessential services.
  • Shifted attention to small, federated, or self-hosted communities when I want connection (or to private, well-defined groups I trust).

    • What I’m not claiming

    Absolute safety. Removing myself from popular social media and aggressively reducing reliance on opaque hardware reduces my exposure and control loss, but no setup is perfect. That every firmware concern is actively exploited against ordinary people today. Many vulnerabilities are exploited primarily in high-value operations. Still, the combination of centralized social data and privileged firmware makes the risk unacceptable to me personally.

    Why this matters for others

    If you value control over your digital life, consider the same trade-offs I did: the convenience of large platforms and modern hardware versus the risk of invisible, hard-to-audit systems and centralized data collection. Even small actions—reducing platform use, using privacy-respecting services, keeping devices up to date, and turning off unnecessary management features—raise the bar for attackers and reduce reliance on closed systems.

    Closing

    I chose to delete myself from major social media and step back from large parts of the public internet because I don’t want my life mediated by corporations I can’t audit and hardware that can be turned into an invisible spying vector. It’s a practical, precautionary move: I’m trading some convenience for more control, fewer permanent traces, and less exposure to opaque, high‑privilege layers that have repeatedly proven risky.
