In Defense of Excellence : Why Classical Music Reigns Supreme

October 25, 2025

A Case for Cultural Hierarchy in an Age of Aesthetic Relativism

Let's dispense with the obligatory niceties: classical music is objectively superior to modern music, and the fact that this statement now sounds controversial rather than self evident is itself a damning indictment of our cultural decline.

The question isn't whether Beethoven's Ninth Symphony towers above the latest Billboard chart-topper—it's why we've become so intellectually dishonest that we pretend otherwise.

The Complexity Chasm

Modern music operates on the level of instant gratification. Three chords, repetitive hooks, lyrics that could fit on a napkin. Classical music, by contrast, demands something our culture has systematically devalued: delayed gratification and intellectual engagement.

A Mahler symphony unfolds over 90 minutes, with themes that recur, transform, and dialogue with each other across movements. A Brahms piano concerto requires the listener to hold multiple melodic lines in their mind simultaneously, to appreciate counterpoint, harmonic progression, and structural architecture that took composers months or years to perfect.

What does modern music offer? The same four-chord progression recycled ad nauseam. Lyrics about breakups delivered with the poetic sophistication of a teenager's diary entry. Production techniques designed explicitly to be catchy literally engineered to hijack your brain's reward system with the subtlety of a slot machine.

The Elite Question: Correlation Isn't Causation (But It's Not Nothing Either)

Yes, classical music audiences skew educated and affluent. The kulturkampf brigade would have you believe this is evidence of gatekeeping or elitism. The more uncomfortable truth? People with refined tastes gravitate toward refined art.

Classical music requires music education to fully appreciate. It demands knowledge of theory, history, and form. This isn't gatekeeping—it's simply acknowledging that some pleasures are acquired tastes that reward study. You wouldn't expect someone to understand higher mathematics without learning the fundamentals. Why should music be different?

The "elites" who populate concert halls aren't there because they're snobs they're there because they've invested the time to understand what makes a piece extraordinary. They can hear when a violinist's intonation is impeccable, when a conductor brings fresh insight to a familiar work, when a pianist's touch reveals depths others miss.

The Craftsmanship Deficit

Consider what it takes to become a classical musician: typically starting before age 10, thousands of hours of practice, mastery of an instrument so complete that technique becomes invisible, knowledge of centuries of repertoire, the ability to sight-read complex scores, and often fluency in multiple languages for vocal works.

Now consider what it takes to become a pop star: Auto-Tune and a good marketing team.

This isn't hyperbole. Modern music production has become so technologically mediated that actual musical skill is increasingly optional. Pitch correction, quantization, loops, samples—the contemporary "musician" is often little more than a brand with a decent voice and a team of ghostwriters and producers doing the heavy lifting.

The Permanence Problem

Here's the ultimate test: which music will still be performed 200 years from now? We know the answer because we have the data. We're still performing Bach, Mozart, Beethoven, Brahms—composers who died centuries ago. Their music has survived wars, revolutions, technological upheavals, and countless changes in fashion.

Will anyone be performing Taylor Swift or Drake in 2225? The very question sounds absurd. Modern music is designed for obsolescence, for the brief dopamine hit before the next manufactured star takes over the algorithm.

Classical music was built for eternity.

The Cathedral vs. The Fast-Food Restaurant

This is ultimately a question of what we value as a civilization. Classical music represents the cathedral: built slowly, deliberately, meant to inspire awe and contemplation, designed to last for generations. Modern music is the fast-food restaurant: mass-produced, optimized for immediate consumption, forgotten as soon as the next meal arrives.

We've chosen comfort over challenge, the immediate over the enduring, the accessible over the transcendent. And we've told ourselves this represents democratic progress rather than cultural impoverishment.

The Uncomfortable Conclusion

The democratization of taste hasn't elevated the masses ; it's lowered the bar for everyone. When we insist that all music is equally valid, we're not being inclusive; we're being intellectually lazy. Some things are better than others. Some art requires more skill, intelligence, and dedication to create and appreciate.

Classical music's association with elites isn't a bug ; it's a feature. It's what happens when a art form refuses to dumb itself down, refuses to pander, refuses to sacrifice excellence at the altar of accessibility.

The concert hall remains one of the last bastions where excellence is still openly celebrated, where we can admit that hierarchy exists in art as in all things, where beauty, complexity, and technical mastery still matter more than marketing budgets and streaming numbers.

If that makes you uncomfortable, perhaps you should ask yourself: when did we decide that defending excellence became elitist, while celebrating mediocrity became virtuous?
