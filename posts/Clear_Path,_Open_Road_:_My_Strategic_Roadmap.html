Clear Path, Open Road : My Strategic Roadmap

November 17, 2025

MISSION BRIEFING: WHY I'VE CHOSEN THIS PATH OF SUFFERING

So there I was, staring at my bank account after looking at the SANS course prices, wondering if I should sell a kidney or just rob a bank (ironically, the latter would give me practical penetration testing experience). But here's the thing about the SANS RedTeam Roadmap combined with OffSec resources: it's like deciding to climb Mount Everest while simultaneously learning to breathe. Unnecessary? Absolutely. Insane? Probably. Worth it? We'll find out when I'm either employed or in a psychiatric ward.

The RedTeam path isn't for the faint of heart or the thin of wallet. It's for those special individuals who look at a perfectly good career in web development and think, "You know what? I'd rather break things professionally and call it security research."

PHASE 1: RECONNAISSANCE - "HELLO, MY NAME IS NOOB"

Every legendary journey begins with reconnaissance. Mine began with Googling "how to hack" like a 12-year-old who just watched Mr. Robot. Smooth operator, I know.

SEC401: Security Essentials - Learning to Crawl Before Running Through Firewalls

This is where SANS politely tells you that everything you thought you knew about security is wrong. It's like believing you're a chef because you can microwave ramen, then Gordon Ramsay walks in and starts screaming about your "idiot sandwich" of security knowledge.

# What I thought security was:
if password == "password123":
    print("Totally secure!")

# What security actually is:
*existential crisis intensifies*

SEC401 covers the fundamentals: cryptography (math that makes you cry), networking (packets go brrr), and incident response (also known as "oh god oh god we've been hacked oh god"). It's 6 days of realizing that every system you've ever trusted is held together by prayers and outdated Java.

OffSec Companion: PEN-100 - Introduction to Cybersecurity

While SANS teaches you the theory, OffSec's PEN-100 introduces you to the practical side of cybersecurity. Think of it as your "Hello World" in hacking. It's cute, it's innocent, and you have no idea what horrors await you in the advanced courses.

# PEN-100 vibes:
print("I'm a hacker now!")
# Narrator: They were not, in fact, a hacker yet.

# But hey, everyone starts somewhere
first_nmap_scan = "nmap 192.168.1.1"
print(f"Running: {first_nmap_scan}")
# *feelings of accomplishment*

PHASE 2: WEAPONIZATION - TURNING KNOWLEDGE INTO EXPLOITS

SEC504: Hacker Tools, Techniques, and Incident Handling

This is where things get spicy. SEC504 is basically "Here's every tool a hacker uses, now YOU be the hacker." It's like becoming Batman, except instead of fighting crime, you're learning how criminals operate so you can... fight crime. The irony is not lost on anyone.

# Learning Metasploit be like:
msf6 > use exploit/everything
msf6 exploit(everything) > set RHOST 192.168.1.1
msf6 exploit(everything) > exploit
[*] Sending exploit...
[*] Got shell? Maybe? Who knows? Time to debug for 3 hours!

You'll learn Nmap, Wireshark, Metasploit, and approximately 47 other tools that you'll struggle to remember the syntax for during the exam. Pro tip: the exam is open book because even SANS knows nobody memorizes this stuff.

OffSec Companion: PEN-200 (OSCP) - Penetration Testing with Kali Linux

NOW we're talking. This is where OffSec takes off the training wheels and pushes you off a cliff. The OSCP is the certification that separates the wannabes from the actual penetration testers. It pairs perfectly with SEC504 because while SANS teaches you the tools, OSCP makes you LIVE with them for months in the lab.

OffSec's motto is "Try Harder™" and boy, do they mean it. Stuck on a box? Try harder. Failed the exam? Try harder. Questioning your existence? TRY. HARDER.

# Every OSCP student's experience:
while not_root:
    enumerate()
    enumerate_again()
    enumerate_some_more()
    check_searchsploit()
    cry()
    enumerate()
    finally_find_vector()
    get_root()
    realize_it_was_sudo_rights_all_along()

The OSCP labs are where you learn that patience is a virtue, enumeration is life, and there's ALWAYS another rabbit hole. It pairs beautifully with SANS courses like wine pairs with cheese, except the wine is tears and the cheese is expensive training materials.

PHASE 3: DELIVERY - ADVANCED ATTACK VECTORS

SEC560: Network Penetration Testing and Ethical Hacking

This is where SANS says, "Remember all those tools from SEC504? Now learn to use them correctly." It's the difference between knowing how to hold a sword and actually knowing how to fence. Spoiler alert: you've been holding it backwards this whole time.

# Network pivoting in theory:
1. Compromise edge system
2. Pivot to internal network
3. Achieve domain dominance
4. Success!

# Network pivoting in practice:
1. Compromise edge system
2. Drop shell immediately
3. Forgot to background the process
4. Connection lost
5. Start over
6. Repeat steps 1-5 approximately 17 times

SEC560 teaches you the art of the network penetration test. You'll learn to think like an attacker, which mostly means thinking "what if I just tried ONE more thing" at 3 AM while questioning why you didn't become an accountant.

SEC542: Web App Penetration Testing and Ethical Hacking

Because apparently, network hacking wasn't enough suffering. Now we get to learn how developers accidentally create security vulnerabilities by existing. Every web app is a potential disaster, and this course proves it.

# SQL Injection - The Developer's Nightmare
userInput = request.form['username']
query = "SELECT * FROM users WHERE username = '" + userInput + "'"

# Famous last words: "What could go wrong?"
# Everything. Everything could go wrong.
# Input: ' OR '1'='1
# Result: *entire database has left the chat*

You'll master OWASP Top 10, Burp Suite (the hacker's Swiss Army knife), and the ancient art of XSS payloads. By the end, you'll never trust a web form again. Online shopping will become an anxiety-inducing security audit.

OffSec Companions: WEB-100, WEB-200, WEB-300 - The Web Security Trilogy

While SANS gives you SEC542, OffSec says "hold my beer" and gives you an ENTIRE web security track. Because one web course is for amateurs.

WEB-100: Introduction to Web Application Security

OffSec's gentle introduction to the beautiful disaster that is web security. You'll learn that every input field is a potential injection point and every developer is one bad day away from creating SQLi vulnerabilities. It's like learning that Santa isn't real, except instead of Santa, it's "secure websites."

# WEB-100 revelations:
secure_website = False  # Always
user_input_is_evil = True  # Forever
trust_nothing = "Especially not forms"

print("Welcome to web security, kid.")
print("Everything is broken. Everything.")
WEB-200 (OSWA): OffSec Web Assessor

Now we're getting serious about web exploitation. This is where you learn that XSS isn't just three random letters, and CSRF isn't a typo. It's where web developers' nightmares become your playground. OSWA pairs perfectly with SEC542, giving you both the theory and the hands-on "figure it out yourself" OffSec experience.

# Web security before WEB-200:
input = user_input
print(input)  # What could go wrong?

# Web security after WEB-200:
*paranoid code review intensifies*
*sanitizes everything*
*validates everything*
*escapes everything*
*still gets hacked because you forgot about deserialization*
WEB-300 (OSWE): Advanced Web Attacks - The Final Web Boss

WEB-300 is where OffSec asks, "You thought you knew web security? Cute." This is whitebox testing on steroids. You get the source code, which sounds like cheating until you realize you now have to READ CODE and find vulnerabilities that aren't obvious. It's like Where's Waldo, except Waldo is a critical RCE vulnerability hidden in 50,000 lines of legacy PHP.

# WEB-300 whitebox testing reality:
receive_source_code()
think_it_will_be_easy()
start_code_review()
*20 hours later*
find_interesting_function()
trace_user_input()
discover_insecure_deserialization()
craft_exploit()
exploit_fails()
review_code_again()
realize_you_misread_the_logic()
fix_exploit()
achieve_rce()
feel_like_hackerman()
*immediately get humbled by next challenge*

PHASE 4: EXPLOITATION - GOING FULL REDTEAM

SEC564: Red Team Operations and Adversary Emulation

This is it. The big leagues. Where you learn to simulate actual nation-state adversaries. You know, the kind of threat actors who have budgets bigger than your student loans and resources that make Hollywood heist movies look underfunded. No pressure.

# RedTeam Rules of Engagement
1. Don't get caught
2. Document everything
3. Don't get caught
4. Stay in scope
5. Seriously, don't get caught
6. Coffee is your friend
7. REALLY don't get caught

SEC564 teaches operational security, adversary tactics (thanks, MITRE ATT&CK framework), and how to think like a sophisticated threat actor. It's like method acting, but for cybercriminals, and somehow legal when you have proper authorization.

OffSec Companion: PEN-300 (OSEP) - Evasion Techniques and Breaching Defenses

Just when you thought you were getting comfortable, OffSec comes back with PEN-300 to remind you that antivirus software exists and it WILL ruin your day. This course is all about evasion techniques, or as I call it, "Advanced Hide and Seek with EDR."

PEN-300 pairs perfectly with SEC564 because while SANS teaches you RedTeam operations and tactics, OffSec teaches you how to actually SURVIVE in a modern enterprise environment where literally everything is trying to detect and kill your implants.

# Traditional exploit execution:
execute_payload.exe
[*] Antivirus: "LOL NO"
[*] EDR: "DETECTED. QUARANTINED. USER MOCKED."
[*] SIEM: "Sending alert to entire SOC team"
[*] You: *sweating profusely*

# After PEN-300:
obfuscate()
encrypt()
process_inject()
memory_patch()
unhook_apis()
disable_etw()
pray_to_security_gods()
execute_payload()
[*] Maybe success? Check C2...
[*] *Beacon connected*
[*] *Achievement unlocked: Actual RedTeamer*

PHASE 5: COMMAND & CONTROL - MAINTAINING ACCESS

SEC660: Advanced Penetration Testing, Exploit Writing, and Ethical Hacking

At this point, SANS basically says, "You know how to break in? Cool. Now learn to break EVERYTHING." This course covers exploit development, which is just a fancy way of saying "professional software breaking with extra steps and assembly code."

# Exploit development workflow:
1. Analyze vulnerable application
2. Reverse engineer binary
3. Craft exploit
4. Test exploit
5. Exploit crashes
6. Debugger shows segfault
7. Question life choices
8. Drink coffee
9. Fix exploit
10. Repeat steps 4-9 until success or retirement

You'll dive deep into buffer overflows, shellcode, and the dark arts of memory corruption. It's like learning brain surgery, except the brain is made of ones and zeros, and your scalpel is a debugger. What could possibly go wrong?

Note: This is where the OffSec courses naturally conclude. SEC660 stands alone in its glory as the pinnacle of low-level exploitation, proving that sometimes SANS just goes places OffSec hasn't ventured into yet. Or maybe OffSec is still recovering from the trauma of writing their existing courses.

FOR610: Reverse Engineering Malware

Because understanding how to break things isn't enough—you need to understand how OTHER PEOPLE break things. This course teaches you to reverse engineer malware, which is essentially digital archaeology except everything you uncover is designed to destroy things.

# Malware analysis in a nutshell:
load_sample_in_isolated_vm()
attach_debugger()
set_breakpoints()
execute()
watch_chaos_unfold()
document_iocs()
write_yara_rules()
realize_malware_author_is_genius()
feel_inadequate()

WHY COMBINE SANS AND OFFSEC? BECAUSE I HATE MONEY AND LOVE PAIN

Here's the thing about combining SANS and OffSec: it's like training for a marathon by also doing CrossFit and learning martial arts. Is it excessive? Yes. Is it expensive? Laughs in student loans. Will it make you incredibly competent? Absolutely.

SANS gives you the theory, the frameworks, and the enterprise perspective. OffSec gives you the practical, hands-on, "figure it out yourself" mentality. Together, they form the ultimate security training combo like peanut butter and jelly, except this sandwich costs approximately $15,000 and takes years to consume.

THE REALITY CHECK: WHAT NOBODY TELLS YOU

The Time Investment

Each SANS course is roughly 6 days of intense training plus exam prep. Each OffSec cert requires hundreds of lab hours. Do the math, and you're looking at roughly 1,000+ hours of study time. That's like working a part-time job, except you're paying THEM and the only compensation is knowledge and crippling impostor syndrome.

The Financial Impact

# Budget calculation:
sans_course_cost = 8000  # per course
offsec_cert_cost = 1500  # per cert

social_life_cost = 0  # because what social life?

total_sans = sans_course_cost * 6  # $48,000
total_offsec = offsec_cert_cost * 6  # $9,000 (PEN-100,200,300 + WEB-100,200,300)

The Mr. Robot Reality Check: Alternative Acquisition Methods

Here's where we address the elephant in the room. You want to be a "real hacker" and you watched Mr. Robot? Then you KNOW there are... alternative methods of acquiring learning materials. I'm not saying you should. I'm not saying I did. I'm just saying that if you're learning penetration testing and you can't figure out how to penetrate a paywall, maybe you're in the wrong field. Just saying.

# Hypothetical alternatives (educational purposes only):
methods = [
    "The Library Genesis Approach",
    "The Torrent Enthusiast Method", 
    "The 'Sharing is Caring' Philosophy",
    "The Academic Research Backdoor",
    "The 'My Friend Has a Copy' Strategy",
    "The 'I'll Buy It Later' Promise"
]

for method in methods:
    print(f"{method}: *wink wink*")
    
print("\nDisclaimer: Support the creators when you can afford it.")
print("But also: You're learning to break into systems.")
print("If you can't figure this out... ¯\_(ツ)_/¯")

Look, the InfoSec community has a complex relationship with piracy. On one hand, we're all about respecting intellectual property and following the law. On the other hand, we're teaching people to break into systems for a living. The cognitive dissonance is chef's kiss.

The truth? Most security researchers started somewhere broke, hungry, and creative about acquiring knowledge. Once you get that sweet RedTeam job and start pulling six figures, THEN you can afford to buy all the courses officially and feel morally superior. Until then? Well, Mr. Robot didn't pay for his education, did he?

Official stance: Buy the courses, support the creators, play by the rules. Unofficial stance: You're literally learning how to break rules professionally. Figure it out. It's part of the training.

# The cycle of security professionals:
1. Start broke, "acquire" learning materials creatively
2. Learn penetration testing
3. Get hired
4. Earn actual money
5. Buy all the courses officially
6. Feel guilty about step 1
7. Help next generation of broke hackers
8. Repeat cycle

# It's the circle of life, InfoSec edition

The Mental Toll

There will be days when you stare at a terminal for 8 hours trying to pop a single shell. There will be nights where you dream in hexadecimal. You'll start seeing buffer overflows in your breakfast cereal. This is normal. Embrace the madness.

LESSONS FROM THE BATTLEFIELD

Lesson 1: Enumeration is King

If I had a dollar for every time the solution was "enumerate more," I could afford another SANS course. That's the real tragedy.

# The eternal cycle:

Lesson 2: Documentation Saves Lives

Nothing is more painful than successfully exploiting a system, losing your shell, and realizing you didn't document HOW you did it. Future you will hate past you with the fury of a thousand suns.

Lesson 3: The Community is Everything

The InfoSec community is simultaneously the most helpful and most sarcastic group of people you'll ever meet. They'll help you solve a problem while also roasting your methodology. It's beautiful, really.

FINAL THOUGHTS: OPERATION CRIMSON COVENANT STATUS REPORT

Am I insane for pursuing this path? Probably. Is it worth it? Ask me when I'm not surviving on instant ramen and energy drinks. Will I regret this decision? Only when I check my bank balance or try to explain to normal people what I do.

But here's the truth: RedTeam operations sit at the intersection of puzzle- solving, creativity, and legitimate chaos. It's one of the few fields where breaking things is not only acceptable—it's your job description. You get paid to think like a criminal without the inconvenient prison time.

The SANS RedTeam Roadmap combined with OffSec certifications isn't just training—it's a transformative experience that will fundamentally change how you view technology, security, and your own capabilities. It's expensive, time- consuming, mentally exhausting, and occasionally soul-crushing.

I wouldn't have it any other way.
