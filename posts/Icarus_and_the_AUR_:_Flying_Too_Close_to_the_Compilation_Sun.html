Icarus and the AUR : Flying Too Close to the Compilation Sun

November 12, 2025

A Confession: I Actually Like Compiling

Look, I'll be honest. There's something deeply satisfying about watching makepkg do its thing. The cascade of compiler flags, the gentle hum of your CPU fans reaching frequencies only dogs can hear, the warm glow of knowing your binary is optimized for YOUR specific microarchitecture (all 0.3% of performance gains). It's therapeutic. It's meditative. It's probably a diagnosable condition.

But sometimes—and I say this with great reluctance—you just need the damn software to work RIGHT NOW. Maybe you're on a deadline. Maybe your laptop's on 15% battery. Maybe you've already spent three hours debugging why some Rust crate won't compile and you're questioning your life choices.

For those moments of weakness (also known as "having things to do"), third-party repositories exist. Consider this your emergency backup plan.

What Even IS Chaotic-AUR? (The Nuclear Option)

Chaotic-AUR is what happens when someone looked at the AUR and thought "You know what this needs? Less character-building suffering." It's a third-party repository that pre-compiles AUR packages so you can install them with pacman instead of watching progress bars like it's 2008.

Yes, I know. Pre-compiled binaries. It feels wrong, doesn't it? Like using an IDE instead of vim, or putting ketchup on a hot dog. But desperate times call for desperate measures, and sometimes that presentation is due in 20 minutes and Chromium is STILL compiling.

It's for people who:

  • Have accidentally scheduled two deadlines simultaneously
  • Don't want their laptop to achieve liftoff during meetings
  • Need software installed before the heat death of the universe
  • Are temporarily willing to sacrifice compiler optimization purity for the ability to, you know, actually use their computer

The Beautiful Agony of Manual Compilation (And Why We Tolerate Alternatives)

Let me paint you a picture: It's 2 AM. You're three hours into compiling ungoogled-chromium. Your room is now a sauna. Your electricity bill will make you cry. But you're LEARNING. You're watching every single object file get compiled. You feel alive. You feel connected to the silicon.

And then it fails at 97% because of some obscure dependency conflict.

This is the Arch Way. This is character development. This is why we're better than Ubuntu users.

But—and hear me out—what if you could experience that joy of building from source 95% of the time, and then have a safety net for when your boss needs something installed in the next 10 minutes? Revolutionary concept: choice without shame.

Setting Up Chaotic-AUR: Embracing Pragmatism

Fine. You've decided to join the dark side. Let's get this over with before you change your mind and decide to compile everything from scratch like a proper masochist.

Step 1: Install the Keyring and Mirrorlist

First, grab their packages. Deep breath. This doesn't make you a bad person. Probably.

sudo pacman -U 'https://cdn-mirror.chaotic.cx/chaotic-aur/chaotic-keyring.pkg.tar.zst' \
'https://cdn-mirror.chaotic.cx/chaotic-aur/chaotic-mirrorlist.pkg.tar.zst'

You're downloading pre-built packages to help you download more pre-built packages. The irony is thicker than your .bashrc file.

Step 2: Edit Your pacman.conf (The Point of No Return)

Open /etc/pacman.conf. I'm assuming you use vim because you have taste. If you use nano, at least you're consistent with your questionable life choices.

sudo vim /etc/pacman.conf

Add these lines at the bottom, preferably while averting your eyes:

[chaotic-aur]
Include = /etc/pacman.d/chaotic-mirrorlist

Save and exit. Vim users: :wq. Nano users: I'm not even going to help you. Figure it out.

Step 3: Update Your Database

sudo pacman -Sy

Watch as thousands of packages flood your database. Feel the weight of all those binaries you didn't personally compile. This is what compromise feels like. Get used to it; adulthood is full of these moments.

Actually Using Chaotic-AUR: When Principles Meet Deadlines

Okay, so now you can install AUR packages like they're regular packages. It feels dirty. It feels wrong. It also takes 5 seconds instead of 5 hours. You do the math.

sudo pacman -S yay

Yes, you're installing an AUR helper from a repo that exists to bypass the AUR building process. The cognitive dissonance is spectacular. You're basically using training wheels to learn how to install training wheels. Just embrace the absurdity.

Want Visual Studio Code without waiting for Electron to compile?

sudo pacman -S visual-studio-code-bin

Installed in seconds. No compilation. No fan orchestra. No questioning whether JavaScript was a mistake (it was, but that's unrelated). Just software. Working. Immediately. Like some kind of... normal operating system.

What's Actually Available? (Your Emergency Stash)

Pretty much everything you'd normally waste time compiling:

  • Browsers: brave-bin, google-chrome (yes, the proprietary shame), vivaldi
  • Development: visual-studio-code-bin, android-studio (because life's too short to compile Android toolchains)
  • Communication: discord, slack-desktop, zoom (for when corporate America demands your presence)
  • Everything else: spotify, basically anything you actually need to use instead of build

Search their offerings with:

pacman -Ss chaotic-aur/

Or visit their website like a civilized human who values their time. Sometimes.

How Chaotic-AUR Actually Works (For the Paranoid Among Us)

Since we're trusting strangers with root access (casual Thursday on Arch Linux), you might want to know who's building these packages.

Chaotic-AUR uses automated build systems that pull PKGBUILDs from the AUR, compile them in clean chroot environments (so they're not contaminated by that weird thing you installed last Tuesday), sign them cryptographically, and serve them via CDN. The whole process is transparent and happens at UFSCar datacenter in Brazil. You can even watch the builds on their GitHub if you enjoy that sort of thing.

It's maintained by volunteers who apparently have nothing better to do than make your life easier. These people are either saints or deeply misunderstand the concept of "hobbies."

BlackArch: When Regular Arch Isn't Edgy Enough

Oh, you want to feel like you're in a hacker movie? Let me introduce you to BlackArch—the repository equivalent of wearing a black hoodie in a dark room while typing furiously. It's Kali Linux's cooler, Arch-using cousin who definitely has opinions about systemd.

What is BlackArch, Realistically?

BlackArch is an unofficial repository focused on penetration testing and security research. It contains over 2,800 tools, which is approximately 2,785 more than you'll ever actually use. But hey, it's nice to have options.

Unlike Chaotic-AUR which just repackages AUR stuff, BlackArch maintains its own PKGBUILDs specifically for security tools. They're organized into categories like "exploitation," "wireless," "forensic"—basically everything you need to either secure a network or get arrested trying.

Setting Up BlackArch (Descending Further)

First, download their setup script. Yes, we're curling and executing random scripts as root AGAIN. Your security professor just had an aneurysm, but this is actually the official method:

curl -O https://blackarch.org/strap.sh

Verify the checksum because we're not complete barbarians:

echo 26849980b35a42e6e192c6d9ed8c46f0d6d06047 strap.sh | sha1sum -c

If that matches, make it executable and run it:

chmod +x strap.sh
sudo ./strap.sh

The script configures everything automatically. It's almost like they expected people to actually use this.

If you're the suspicious type who reads scripts before executing them (good instinct, by the way), here's the manual method:

curl -O https://blackarch.org/keyring/blackarch-keyring.pkg.tar.xz
curl -O https://blackarch.org/keyring/blackarch-keyring.pkg.tar.xz.sig
gpg --keyserver keyserver.ubuntu.com --recv-keys 4345771566D76038C7FEB43863EC0ADBEA87E4E3
gpg --verify blackarch-keyring.pkg.tar.xz.sig
sudo pacman -U blackarch-keyring.pkg.tar.xz

Then add to /etc/pacman.conf:

[blackarch]
Server = https://www.mirrorservice.org/sites/blackarch.org/blackarch/$repo/os/$arch

Pick a mirror from their mirror list that's geographically close to you. Latency matters, even when downloading exploit frameworks.

Update your system:

sudo pacman -Sy

Using BlackArch: The Actually Fun Part

Want to install everything? (Don't. It's 30GB+ and you'll never use most of it. But I can't stop you.)

sudo pacman -S blackarch

Better idea—install by category:

sudo pacman -S blackarch-exploitation
sudo pacman -S blackarch-wireless
sudo pacman -S blackarch-forensic

Or just install what you actually need:

sudo pacman -S nmap metasploit john aircrack-ng

Congratulations, you now have the tools to either legally pentest systems you have permission to test, or illegally ruin your life. Please choose wisely. Prison doesn't have good package managers.

BioArchLinux: For the Scientifically Inclined

You thought BlackArch was niche? Hold my pipette. BioArchLinux is a repository so specialized that even mentioning it makes people check if you're lost.

What is BioArchLinux?

BioArchLinux is a repository containing over 3,770 bioinformatics packages maintained by actual scientists from the University of Nottingham, Shenzhen Research Institute, Ghent University, and other places where people have PhDs and concerns about p-values.

These are researchers who realized that spending 6 hours compiling R/Bioconductor packages is time not spent on actual research. Which is ironic because they're the only field where "I was compiling" is actually a valid excuse.

The repository uses automated tools (Lilac and archrepo2) to track upstream releases. It's even published in peer-reviewed journals (DOI: 10.1093/bioinformatics/btaf106), because scientists do everything properly, including their Linux repositories.

Installing BioArchLinux

Add to /etc/pacman.conf:

[bioarchlinux]
Server = https://repo.bioarchlinux.org/$arch

Then update and install the keyring:

sudo pacman -Sy && sudo pacman -S bioarchlinux-keyring

Yes, you add the repo FIRST, then install the keyring FROM that repo. It's backwards and weird, but it works. Scientists are creative like that.

Is BioArchLinux Worth It?

Short answer: Only if you're actually doing bioinformatics.

Long answer: If you're sequencing genomes, analyzing proteomics data, or doing anything where words like "BLAST," "Bowtie2," or "Galaxy" mean something other than space stuff, then yes, absolutely. You get 3,770+ specialized packages without maintaining them yourself.

Use it if:

  • You're a bioinformatics researcher/student
  • You work with genomics, proteomics, metabolomics, or other -omics
  • You value reproducibility (your paper reviewers certainly do)
  • You'd rather analyze data than compile toolchains
  • You understand what a phylogenetic tree is (and it's not landscaping)

Don't use it if:

  • You just collect repositories like Pokémon cards
  • You think "DNA sequencing" is something from CSI
  • Your genome analysis consists of 23andMe results
  • You don't want 3,770 packages you'll never touch

They even provide Docker images, WSL support, and a custom ISO. These people are thorough. Probably comes with the whole "scientific method" thing.

Third-Party Repositories: The Trust Fall Exercise

Before you go wild adding every repository mentioned on Reddit, let's talk about trust. The Arch Wiki has excellent documentation on unofficial user repositories that's actually worth reading (unlike most documentation).

Here's what you're trusting these repositories with:

Chaotic-AUR: Automated build system at UFSCar datacenter in Brazil. Builds AUR packages in clean chroots, signs them, serves them. Transparent process, GitHub-visible builds, maintained by volunteers who apparently enjoy infrastructure management. Everything's cryptographically signed, which means tampering is difficult (not impossible, but difficult).

BlackArch: Maintained by the BlackArch team who package security tools. They write their own PKGBUILDs rather than pulling from AUR. Organized, categorized, and surprisingly professional for a project that sounds like an edgy teenager named it. Over 2,800 tools curated by people who actually use them for legal purposes (presumably).

BioArchLinux: International team spanning five countries. Automated builds tracked on GitHub at BioArchLinux/Packages. Published in Bioinformatics and F1000Research journals. When your repository has DOI citations, you're doing something right. As legitimate as third-party repos get, academically verified and everything.

Safe Usage: Not Shooting Yourself in the Foot

Third-party repositories are convenient. They're also potential security nightmares if you're careless. Here's how to use them without becoming a cautionary tale:

1. Understand What You're Installing

That "network analyzer" from BlackArch? Read what it does BEFORE installing. Some tools are designed to do questionable things, legally in pentesting contexts, illegally everywhere else.

2. Verify Package Signatures

If pacman complains about invalid signatures, that's not a minor inconvenience. That's a STOP EVERYTHING alarm. Someone either screwed up or something worse is happening.

sudo pacman-key --verify package.pkg.tar.zst.sig

3. Read What Gets Installed

Don't just spam Y through package installations. Read what's being installed, updated, removed. Optional dependencies matter. That "optional" telemetry package? Maybe skip it.

4. Keep Everything Updated

Security vulnerabilities get patched. Running outdated packages because you're lazy is how you become a statistic.

sudo pacman -Syu

Run this regularly. Like, actually regularly, not "I'll get to it eventually" regularly.

5. Use a Firewall

Especially with BlackArch tools. Many are designed to communicate over networks. Make sure they only do it when YOU want them to, not when someone else wants them to.

sudo pacman -S ufw
sudo ufw enable

6. Legal Considerations (The "Don't Go to Jail" Section)

Many BlackArch tools are for penetration testing. Using them on systems you don't own or have written permission to test is ILLEGAL. Not "against TOS" illegal. Not "might get banned" illegal. Actual "federal crime" illegal.

Get permission. In writing. With signatures. From people with authority to give that permission. Being able to hack something doesn't mean you should. This is the difference between cybersecurity professionals and criminals.

7. When Things Go Wrong (Damage Control)

Installed something sketchy? Remove it immediately and thoroughly:

sudo pacman -Rns suspicious-package

The -s removes dependencies, -n removes config files. Nuclear option, but sometimes necessary.

Want to remove an entire repository?

# Remove Chaotic-AUR
sudo pacman -Rns chaotic-keyring chaotic-mirrorlist

# Remove BlackArch  
sudo pacman -Rns blackarch-keyring

# Remove BioArchLinux
sudo pacman -Rns bioarchlinux-keyring

# Refresh everything
sudo pacman -Syu

Then comment out the repository in /etc/pacman.conf.

Combining Repositories: The Jenga Approach

Using multiple third-party repositories? Bold. Stupid? Possibly. But if you're going to do it, at least do it right.

Repository order in /etc/pacman.conf matters. First listed gets priority. Here's a sane configuration:

[core]
Include = /etc/pacman.d/mirrorlist

[extra]
Include = /etc/pacman.d/mirrorlist

[chaotic-aur]
Include = /etc/pacman.d/chaotic-mirrorlist

[blackarch]
Server = https://www.mirrorservice.org/sites/blackarch.org/blackarch/$repo/os/$arch

[bioarchlinux]
Server = https://repo.bioarchlinux.org/$arch

Official repos first (obviously), then third-party in descending order of how much you trust them not to break everything. Generally, these three don't conflict since they serve different purposes. Generally. Murphy's Law still applies.

The "But I Like Compiling!" Crowd (My People)

There's always someone who says "But compiling gives me architecture-specific optimizations!" Yes. Fellow traveler. I too enjoy the ritual. The waiting. The anticipation. The validation that your time is worth something because you EARNED that binary.

But let's be real: those optimizations save you maybe 0.003 seconds on program startup. You spent 2 hours compiling. The math doesn't math, no matter how much we want it to.

Here's my compromise: Compile what matters. Use repos for what doesn't. Your custom kernel? Compile it. Your window manager? Compile it. Your lovingly crafted dwm build with exactly 473 lines of patches? DEFINITELY compile it.

But Chromium? Electron apps? That proprietary garbage you need for work? Just... use the binary. Save your compilation energy for things that matter. Life's too short to compile everything, even if we wish it weren't.

The Verdict: Pragmatism vs. Purity

Look, I get it. We use Arch because we like control. We like understanding our systems. We like the satisfaction of building things ourselves. Using pre-compiled binaries feels like cheating. Like taking the elevator instead of the stairs. Like—dare I say it—using Ubuntu.

But here's the thing: having the OPTION doesn't make you weak. Having an emergency backup for when compilation isn't feasible doesn't invalidate your l33t status. It makes you practical. Functional. Able to actually get work done when work needs doing.

Install these repos. Use them when you need them. Compile when you want to. It's called having a mature relationship with your operating system.

And if anyone gives you grief about using pre-compiled packages, just remind them that they probably installed their system from an ISO instead of bootstrapping from scratch like the TRUE Arch purists of legend.

Final Thoughts: Choose Your Own Adventure

These repositories exist because sometimes—just sometimes—getting stuff done is more important than the aesthetic pleasure of watching gcc scroll by. I know. Heresy. But true.

Use them wisely. Use them sparingly. Use them when your deadline is in 20 minutes and your package has been compiling for 3 hours. But also: enjoy compiling when you can. There's something meditative about it. Something deeply satisfying about creating your own binaries.

We're Arch users. We make our own choices. Sometimes that choice is spending all night compiling. Sometimes it's downloading a binary and moving on with our lives. Both are valid. Neither makes you less of a Linux user.

Now if you'll excuse me, I have a custom kernel to compile. With patches. That I wrote myself. Because some of us still have standards.

(But I'll probably use Chaotic-AUR for Spotify. Because I'm not an animal.)
