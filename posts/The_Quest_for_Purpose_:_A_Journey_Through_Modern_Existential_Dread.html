The Quest for Purpose : A Journey Through Modern Existential Dread

October 24, 2025

In Which Humans Climb Mountains, Start Podcasts, and Generally Panic About Meaning

Once upon a time, in the grand cosmic joke we call existence, humans discovered something terrifying: they could think about thinking. Unlike the blessed pigeons pecking at bread crumbs or cats knocking things off tables with pure, purposeless joy, humans looked up at the stars and asked, "But why though?"

And thus began humanity's most epic quest more dramatic than Odysseus sailing home, more obsessive than Ahab chasing his whale. The search for Purpose with a capital P, that golden fleece of meaning that apparently everyone needs or they'll simply combust into existential despair.

The Ancient Prophecy

The old myths understood something we keep forgetting: humans are really bad at just existing.

Sisyphus had his boulder. Prometheus had his liver-eating eagle situation. Even the gods themselves were running around having affairs and starting wars because apparently eternal bliss gets boring without a hobby. The ancients knew that humans need a quest, a reason, a thing otherwise they'd just sit around realizing how weird it is that we're conscious meat puppets on a rock floating through infinite space.

So we built temples. Wrote epic poems. Invented cults, philosophies, and self-help books. All different ways of answering the same question : "What am I supposed to be doing with this absurd gift of awareness?"

The Modern Hero's Journey (AKA: The Hustle)

Fast forward to today, and nothing has changed except now we have Instagram to document our existential crises.

Watch the modern human in their natural habitat: They'll wake up at 5 AM to run marathons, adopt minimalism, start dropshipping businesses, learn Portuguese, ferment their own kombucha, and launch a podcast about their "journey"—all before lunch. Why? Because the alternative is sitting quietly with their thoughts, and that way lies madness.

Purpose has become the dragon everyone must slay, except the dragon is invisible and might not exist and also you need to post about slaying it on LinkedIn or it doesn't count.

The Lengths We'll Go (Spoiler: It's Ridiculous)

Here's the truly mythical part: humans will do literally anything to feel purposeful. And I mean anything.

They'll spend years getting degrees they don't care about. Work jobs that slowly devour their souls like Kronos eating his children. Move to different continents. Join movements. Start movements. Quit movements when those movements don't give them enough meaning. They'll have children (creating new humans who will also need purpose talk about a pyramid scheme). They'll climb actual mountains just to stand on top and think, "Yes, this pile of rocks gives my life meaning."

Some will dedicate themselves to helping others. Some to accumulating wealth. Some to art, science, or arguing with strangers on the internet. The what doesn't really matter it's the having a purpose that counts.

It's like we're all on individual hero's journeys, except there's no mentor, the map is missing, and we're not entirely sure what we're supposed to be heroic about. But by the gods, we'll figure it out or die trying. (Preferably die while looking purposeful.)

The Cosmic Joke

Here's what makes this whole situation mythically absurd : purpose is both completely made up and absolutely essential.

Unlike a phoenix that knows its job is to burn and be reborn, or a dragon that simply vibes in its cave guarding gold, humans have to invent their own reasons for existing. We're the only creature cursed with enough intelligence to need meaning but not enough wisdom to know what that meaning should be.

So we make it up. We tell ourselves stories. We become heroes of our own epic tales even if those tales are about selling insurance or collecting vintage stamps or making the perfect sourdough starter. We turn our random acts into quests, our mistakes into character development, our suffering into "growth opportunities."

And somehow, this complete fabrication becomes real. The purpose we invent starts to actually work. People will genuinely find meaning in the strangest things. They'll spend decades perfecting a craft nobody asked for. Fight for causes that history might forget. Love people who'll die anyway. Build things that'll crumble.

The Great Paradox

The most ridiculous part? We need this fiction.

Strip away someone's purpose and watch them unravel faster than Theseus's thread. Humans without purpose don't just feel sad they feel erased, like side characters in someone else's story. We'd rather have a terrible purpose than no purpose at all. At least Sisyphus had his boulder. At least Prometheus knew why that eagle kept showing up.

This is why people join cults, start wars, devote themselves to ridiculous causes, or become extremely serious about their fantasy football leagues. The human brain looks at the void of meaninglessness and says, "Absolutely not." I'm making up a reason to exist, and I'm making it everyone's problem.

The Moral of Our Myth

So here we are, billions of humans on a spinning rock, each desperately trying to matter. Some find their purpose in grand ambitions. Others in small, quiet moments. Most of us stumble through life hoping we're the protagonist and not just background noise in the universe's elevator music.

And maybe that's okay.

Maybe the search itself is the point. Maybe we're all just Sisyphus, rolling our boulders up different mountains, and the secret is that the boulder is the purpose. Maybe purpose isn't something you find it's something you drag behind you, getting heavier and more absurd with each passing year, until one day you look back and realize it's shaped your entire existence.

Or maybe, just maybe, we're all taking this way too seriously, and the pigeons pecking at breadcrumbs have it figured out better than we ever will.

Either way, we're stuck with this need for meaning. Might as well make it a good story.
